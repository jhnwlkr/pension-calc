1 Monte Carlo Simulation

The calculator runs up to 2,000 independent simulations (1,000 by default) of your retirement. Each simulation draws a different sequence of market returns and inflation shocks, producing a full distribution of outcomes rather than a single "average" projection.

Results are reported at five percentile bands — the 5th, 25th, 50th (median), 75th and 95th percentiles of the outcome distribution. The median (50th percentile) is the headline figure shown on the summary cards. The 5th percentile represents a severe but historically plausible scenario; the 95th percentile a favourable one.

  • Initialise pots — each simulation starts from your current pot values, applying any pre-retirement contributions up to the retirement date.
  • Draw retirement pot — at the retirement date, the simulation records each pot's value (after any PCLS deduction) as the starting drawdown balance.
  • Annual drawdown — each year a return pair (equity + bond) is sampled at random from the historical dataset. Growth is applied, then net target income is withdrawn. Cash pots are drawn first, then pension pots.
  • Apply guardrails — if enabled, spending is cut when the pot falls below 80% of its retirement-date value (see Section 4).
  • Record outcome — the final pot value at the target end age is recorded. A simulation where the pot reaches zero before end age counts as a "pot depleted" outcome.
Why not use average returns? Using a single average return ignores sequence-of-returns risk — the danger that poor returns arriving early in retirement cause permanent damage even if long-run averages recover. Monte Carlo simulation captures this effect.

2 Historical Return Data

Returns are drawn by historical bootstrapping: each simulated year's returns are taken as a complete (equity, bond) pair from the historical record, preserving the natural correlation between asset classes that existed in that calendar year. This is sometimes called the "block bootstrap" or "historical simulation" method.

The dataset spans 1900 to 2025 — 126 annual observations of real-world UK/global market conditions including the Great Depression, World War II, the 1970s stagflation, the dot-com crash, the 2008 financial crisis and the 2022 bond crash.

No smoothing or fitting. The raw historical observations are sampled directly — no normal distribution is fitted and no return is artificially capped or floored. The extreme years (e.g. equities −51% in the early 1930s, or bonds −22% in 2022) remain in the dataset and can appear in any simulation.

Each pot's blended return scales with its equity/bond allocation. A pot set to 80% equities receives a blended return of 0.8 × equity return + 0.2 × bond return for that simulated year. Changing a pot's equity percentage only affects that pot — other pots in the same simulation keep their own allocated returns.

The deterministic (bear/base/bull) projections shown on the Pot Balance chart use the long-run mean of the historical series as the base case, ±2 percentage points for the bull and bear scenarios.

3 Stochastic Inflation

Inflation is not fixed at your input rate. Each simulated year adds a small random shock to the base inflation you set, drawn from a normal distribution (standard deviation ≈ 0.8 percentage points). This models the realistic uncertainty in future price levels.

In years where the sampled equity return falls below −10% — implying a significant market crash — an additional "stagflation" shock is added. This reflects the empirical tendency for inflation to remain elevated or spike during periods of financial stress (as observed in the 1970s and after 2021). The stagflation add-on is drawn from a half-normal with mean ≈ 1% and is only positive — it can make inflation worse during crashes, never better.

Inflation is constrained to a minimum of 0.5% per year — deflation scenarios are excluded because the dataset does not contain enough deflationary observations to model reliably, and sustained deflation would typically be accompanied by economic conditions not captured by the equity/bond return history alone.

Your target income is by default inflation-linked — it rises each year in line with that simulation's cumulative inflation. You can disable this in the "Drawdown & Income" settings if you prefer a fixed nominal income target.

4 Guyton-Klinger Guardrails

The Guyton-Klinger guardrail system is an optional spending-flexibility rule that automatically cuts withdrawals in bad market conditions to reduce the risk of running out of money.

The calculator implements a simplified version of the lower guardrail: if, at any year in retirement, the median pension pot across all simulations falls below 80% of the value it had at retirement, all simulations in that year apply a 10% spending cut to the amount withdrawn from the pension pot. The cut applies to the pension pot withdrawal only — income from other sources (state pension, DB pensions, employment) is unaffected.

What this means in practice: If markets fall sharply early in retirement and your median pot contracts significantly, the calculator will reduce your modelled spending by 10% for that year. The "% of scenarios where guardrail triggered" statistic in the results tells you how often this occurs across your 1,000 simulations.

The guardrail is a planning heuristic — it does not guarantee you will not run out of money. It does, however, substantially improve long-run sustainability compared to a rigid fixed-withdrawal strategy in most historical scenarios.

5 Sequence of Returns Risk Stress Test

The Sequence of Returns Risk (SORR) stress test overlays a synthetic market crash at the start of retirement on top of the deterministic projection, to illustrate how poor early returns can permanently damage a portfolio even when long-run averages are adequate.

When enabled, the stress test replaces the first N years of retirement (configurable, 1–10 years) with a user-specified annual return (e.g. −25%), then reverts to your selected nominal return rate for the remainder of retirement. The accumulation phase is unchanged — the crash starts at retirement age.

How to read the results: The red dashed line on the Pot Balance chart shows the SORR scenario pot balance. The year-by-year table shows, for each year of retirement: the return rate applied, the £ gain or loss on the pot from that return, the resulting pot balance, the annual pension withdrawal drawn, and whether the Guyton-Klinger guardrail fired that year.

The guardrail in the SORR scenario works identically to the main simulation: if the pot falls below 80% of its retirement-date value in a given year, a flat 10% reduction is applied to the pension withdrawal target for that year only. The reduction does not compound — each year is assessed independently.

This is a deterministic scenario, not a probability estimate. It answers the question "what would happen to my specific plan under this specific crash?" rather than providing a probability distribution. Use the Monte Carlo tab for probabilistic outcomes.

6 UK Income Tax Modelling

All income is taxed using England/Wales/Northern Ireland rates (Scottish rates are not currently modelled). Tax is computed annually within each simulation using the parameters below.

The Personal Allowance taper applies: for income over £100,000 the allowance is reduced by £1 for every £2 of earnings above that threshold, creating an effective 60% marginal rate between £100,000 and £125,140.

Fiscal drag is modelled explicitly: tax band thresholds are held fixed at their 2024/25 nominal values throughout the projection. This is the current government policy (frozen thresholds until at least 2028). As your income grows with inflation each year, a larger proportion falls into higher bands — exactly replicating the real-world drag effect.

Income sources are stacked for tax purposes each simulation-year in the following order: (1) other earned/property/dividend income, (2) DB pension income, (3) pension pot withdrawals (UFPLS taxable portion or full drawdown), (4) state pension. The gross withdrawal required from the pension pot is back-calculated from the net target income and the applicable marginal rate.

Income used for annual allowance carry-forward calculations (relevant to pension contributions during the accumulation phase) uses the simplified annual allowance rules: £60,000 per year (current limit) with up to three years' carry-forward of unused allowance at the rate applicable in each earlier year.

7 Tax-Free Cash: PCLS & UFPLS

Two tax-efficient withdrawal strategies are supported:

  • Pension Commencement Lump Sum (PCLS) — a one-off, fully tax-free cash payment taken at retirement. The maximum is the lesser of: 25% of the pot, £268,275 (the Lump Sum Allowance as of 2024/25), or the elected percentage. After the PCLS is deducted, all future drawdown from the pot is taxed as income. The PCLS is excluded from the tax-free figure if the pot value would imply an entitlement in excess of the Lump Sum Allowance.
  • Uncrystallised Funds Pension Lump Sum (UFPLS) — each year, 25% of the gross withdrawal is free of income tax and 75% is taxable. This spreads the tax-free element over the entire drawdown phase, which is often more tax-efficient for those whose income would otherwise exceed higher-rate thresholds. The tax calculations account for the tax-free portion when computing the gross withdrawal required.
Lump Sum Allowance (LSA): The LSA of £268,275 is the lifetime cap on tax-free lump sums from pensions (replacing the old Lifetime Allowance). The calculator enforces this limit: if the PCLS election would exceed the LSA, the tax-free amount is capped at £268,275 and remaining funds are drawn as taxable income.

8 State Pension

The State Pension is entered as a fixed annual amount in today's money. Within each simulation, it is uplifted each year by that simulation's cumulative inflation factor (i.e. it is modelled as fully inflation-linked, consistent with the triple lock's inflation element).

The full new State Pension for 2024/25 is £11,502 per year (£221.20 per week). You can enter any amount including a partial entitlement. The calculator does not project your National Insurance record or future State Pension entitlement — you should check your forecast at gov.uk/check-state-pension.

State Pension income is not subject to NI but is counted as taxable income when stacking income sources for income tax purposes. It is however typically offset against your target drawdown income, reducing the amount that needs to be withdrawn from your pension pots.

Triple lock not modelled. The triple lock policy (increases by the highest of inflation, earnings growth, or 2.5%) is not projected forward. The inflation-linking assumption used is conservative relative to the full triple lock and is appropriate for planning purposes.

9 ISA & Lifetime ISA

Two types of tax-advantaged savings accounts are modelled in addition to pension pots:

Stocks & Shares ISA: Annual contributions up to the ISA allowance (currently £20,000 per year) grow free of income tax and capital gains tax. The projected value at retirement compounds at an equity-blend return (configurable per ISA). Withdrawals are fully tax-free and are applied before pension pot withdrawals in each simulation year, reducing the taxable income drawn from pensions.

Lifetime ISA (LISA): The government adds a 25% bonus on contributions up to £4,000 per year (max bonus £1,000/year) until age 50. The LISA can only be used for retirement (age 60+) without penalty, so the bonus is modelled as additional balance accumulating at the same equity-blend return. Contributions and bonuses compound from the contribution start date. Withdrawals at retirement are entirely tax-free.

The annual allowance warnings are shown if your planned ISA contributions exceed £20,000/year or LISA contributions exceed £4,000/year — the hard limits set by HMRC.

Plain cash savings and cash pots (e.g. an inheritance arriving at a future age) are also modelled. Cash pots earn the bond return component of the blended rate (reflecting savings account or cash-equivalent growth) or a fixed 0% real return depending on your settings.

11 DB Pensions & Other Income

Defined Benefit (DB) pensions are modelled as a fixed annual income in today's money, starting at a specified age. Within each simulation, the DB income is uplifted annually by that year's simulated inflation, consistent with statutory DB revaluation (subject to caps which are not modelled here — the inflation-linking is treated as uncapped).

DB income is taxable in the same way as pension income. It is applied before pot withdrawals, reducing the gross amount that must be drawn from defined contribution pots.

Other income sources — employment/self-employment income, rental income, dividends, and savings interest — can also be entered along with their expected duration. Each income type receives the correct tax treatment:

From April 2027 the Personal Allowance no longer flows to savings, property, or dividend tiers (Finance Bill 2025-26 ordering rules); the PSA and dividend allowance continue to apply.

12 Assumptions & Limitations

The following simplifications and assumptions apply to all projections:

  • Tax bands are frozen at 2024/25 nominal values. No future Budget changes are anticipated. This is a conservative (slightly pessimistic) assumption consistent with current government policy.
  • National Insurance is excluded from retirement income tax calculations (State Pension and pension drawdown are not subject to NI). NI is applied to employment/SE income during accumulation where relevant.
  • Scottish income tax rates are not modelled. Scottish taxpayers with different rate bands may see materially different tax outcomes.
  • Annual allowance tapering for very high earners (adjusted income above £260,000) is not modelled. Contributions are assumed to be within the standard or carry-forward allowance.
  • Sequence of returns during pre-retirement is not simulated stochastically — the accumulation chart uses a fixed deterministic return. Only the post-retirement drawdown phase uses Monte Carlo.
  • Currency risk is not modelled. The equity return series reflects a broad market blend; portfolio currency exposure is not separated.
  • Behavioural risks — panic selling, market timing, switching to cash — are not captured. The model assumes the configured equity/bond split is maintained throughout.
  • Inheritance tax and gifting are not modelled. Remaining pension pot at the target end age is shown but no estate planning tax treatment is applied.
  • Platform fees and fund costs are not deducted. You should reduce your expected equity return assumption to account for your portfolio's ongoing charge figure (OCF) and platform fees (typically 0.1–0.5% per year combined).
  • Couple modelling treats both partners' pots independently but combines income for household drawdown calculations. Partner survivorship is not separately modelled.

13 Privacy & Analytics

Your financial data never leaves your device. All calculations run entirely in your browser. No pension amounts, dates of birth, income figures or any other financial inputs are ever transmitted anywhere.

When you click Calculate, an anonymous event is sent to help us understand how the tool is used in aggregate. It contains only:

  • A random anonymous ID generated in your browser (no name, email or personal details)
  • Your country, detected automatically from your connection by Cloudflare
  • Which tab was active and which tabs you visited during your session
  • Whether you are on a mobile or desktop device
  • The website that referred you (e.g. Google), or “direct”

No financial data, no settings values, no dates of birth, no names are ever collected. The anonymous ID is stored in your browser’s local storage under the key pension-client-id and never leaves your computer. You can delete it at any time by clearing your browser’s site data.

Analytics data is processed by Cloudflare Analytics Engine and is used solely to count usage and improve the tool. For privacy queries or to request deletion of your anonymous ID, contact privacy@retirecalc.uk.
